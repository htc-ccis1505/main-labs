So far, you've practiced writing scripts that carry out short sequences of commands. One thing that all of these scripts have in common is that they run every single one of their blocks, no matter what. But what if we wanted to do something only sometimes? Let's look again at the examples from the video:

Bill's example:
   If it's raining, then grab a jacket

If we put that into our leave the house routine, it might go something like this:
- grab the keys
- put on shoes
- if it's raining, (then) grab a jacket
- go out the door

The most imporant thing to notice in this example is that you always will grab the keys, put on shoes, and go out the door. However, it is only sometimes - when it's raining - that you will you grab a jacket. The if statement allows us to do something (like grab a jacket) conditionally. We only do that thing when the statement following the if is true. If it is not true (if it is false), then we skip it and move on to the next step.

Saloni's example:
if it's a weekday, then we go to school
otherwise (else), we stay at home and have fun

It's similar, but a little bit differnt. This is called an if/else statement - the "otherwise" is the else. With these statements you will always do one thing or the other. In the example, you will either go to school or you will stay home and have fun. This is different from the simple if, where you might do something or you might do nothing at all.

Take a moment to let this settle in. Think of some examples of things you might do "if", and decide whether those are best represented as simple if conditions or if they are if/else conditions. We use these conditional statements a lot in programming, so it's good to first get a good sense of how they work in just your every day life.
